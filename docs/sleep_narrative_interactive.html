A-REM-ian nights

Visualising two years of sleep-data, proving and (mostly) disproving my own sleep related theories

I can never overstate how much I love sleep. I obsessively think about how it is influenced by everything through every day, and how to perfect it. This motivated me to pull all the sleep data that was available from my fitness tracker for the last 2 years, and dig through it to find some clues.

01

The Big Picture

Observing overall sleep patterns

The first thing I did was to make a visual with EVERYTHING in it, so I could look for some meta patterns. A special feature of this analysis is the correlation with my monthly subscription to female biology - periods, endearingly referred to as Irma henceforth. I was convinced that the physical and emotional distress during that time of the month have a negative effect on my sleep quality. Though I could only show one year at a time with this circular grid, its a full cycle of all seasons and a pretty good starting point.

Sleep performance in 2025

• Each cell on the grid represents one night of the year.
• The colour of each cell is that night's sleep quality score: the bluer the better, the oranger the poorer.
• The 12 months are angular slices and the concentric rings show the 7 days of the week.
• And the dots in each cell represent the phases of Irma - The red dots of bloody days are dated accurately. The others are approximations with PMS as ~7 days before the day Irma arrived, Eggy as ~7 days before PMS and the rest are Free.

Sleep Quality Distribution

A pie chart breaking down the proportion of excellent, good, fair, and poor sleep nights across the entire dataset.

One of the theories I had before the analysis was that I have almost as many poor days as I have good. I was pleasantly surprised to see the low share of poor days, and the remarkably high share of good days. However, the latter mostly ranges just between "Fair" (70s) and "Good" (80s). I kind of knew this which the data now confirmed.

96 is the highest ever I ever had, on 4 days in the entire dataset of 700 days.
"Very Good" (90-95) or "Excellent" (>95) was a rare black swan sighting.
And 100 is just Bigfoot.

Best and Worst Days

Candlestick chart showing the bed time and waking time pattern.

Tempering my ambitions a bit, I want to find the recipe for the 85+, as that is more recurring and concurs with my feeling of a satisfactory sleep.

The lower end definitely has had erratic timings and durations. I can identify many of those were dates when I was travelling. Something to probe in future analysis on this topic.

The higher scores have roughly consistent bedtimes of around 11-12 pm and wake time of 8-9 am.

I’m not sure if that’s just my body clock, or Garmin in general rewards this particular pattern to all its users. But it's a good lead to explore more.

02

Weekly Waves

How days of the week shape sleep

My hypothesis before I started the visualisations was that weekends have better sleep because there’s no work stress and plenty of time to sleep in etc. Then I saw the ‘everything’ chart (the circular calendar grid) above, the outer rings seemed to have more orange in them. So I unfurled the rings into bar and line charts and plotted the scores split through each day of the week.

Weekly Sleep Quality Distribution

Horizontal bar chart comparing sleep quality for each day of the week.

Weekdays are clearly doing better than the weekends. It becomes more obvisous when you flatten the bars to Quality intervals. And on second thoughts, weekends are not surprising because there is indulgent dining, physical tiredness, and late bed times. Also, many travel days coincide with weekends more than weekdays.

8/10 of the 90+ scores are on weekdays.

Note to self - Investigate the extra orange on Tuesday. Maybe its mid-week work fatigue during some specific weeks.

Weekly Mean of all Sleep Stages

- Trend lines comparing the mean sleep stages - Deep, Light, REM and Total for each day of the week.
- Top line covers the mean sleep score of each day of the week

Tuesday is only one with mean sleep >8 hours AND sleep score >70.
Deep and REM are roughly consistent at ~1 and ~2 hours, but still marginally better on weekdays.

Garmin says deep sleep is important for physical recovery, whereas REM is good for improving memory function.

I don't mind the extraordinarily long REM. I love that rush of visiting another world, even though I forget the actual dreams soon after waking up. But I think it cuts into my deep-sleep. Increasing physical activities/workout could pull in some more Deep, and get me that 100 I dream about.

03

Seasonal Shifts

How months and seasons influence rest

So far my undisputed theory was that I sleep better in spring, summer, and autumn compared to winter. Even though winter has that whole 'cozy holidays' personality, my preferred sleep condition is that it should be cool and breezy, enough to pull up a thin blanket but keep one foot out of the blanket.

Monthly Sleep Quality Distribution

Horizontal bar chart comparing quality across all months.
*Feb and March have many missing days in the dataset, so these 2 bars might be skewed.

Well, hypothesis confirmed about warmer months winning on quality. One of the two things that I can think of that moves in a similar path across months is Temperature, as I previously suspected.

May to August have ~14% of all poor sleep days (under 40), and ~41% of all good sleep days (above 80).

The second one besides temperature, is the higher amount of outdoors physical activity that likely contributes to better sleep quality in warmer months.

Monthly Mean of all Sleep Stages

- Trend lines comparing the mean sleep stages - Deep, Light, REM and Total for each month.
- Top line covers the mean sleep score of each month.

Here's another quirky seasonal insight - the quality-score trend is almost an inverse of the sleep-duration trend. Even though there more hours of sleep during the colder months, the score is still better in warmer ones.

July has the highest mean score (73.17) with highest deep and REM mean durations.
December has the highest total sleep duration (8.23 hrs), but has the second lowest mean score (62.55).

It should be noted that this total sleep duration is the total time spent in bed, including the restless awake moments. This explains the poor quality of these longsleep/low score days.

04

Irma's Journey

The cycle of sleep through cycles of biology

The pièce de résistance of this whole study - how has my menstrual cycle been affecting my sleep. I made another chart similar to the first circular calendar, but this time with more focus on comparing the cycle days with bar sizes instead of just the colour gradients.

Radial Phase Chart

The red bloody Irma days are dated accurately, naturally.
The others dates are approximations:
• PMS is ~7 days before the day Irma arrived
• Eggy as ~7 days before PMS
• The rest are Free.

The red nights actually have longer bars! This goes contrary to my hypothesis. Even on the the first 3 days, the bars are mostly long despite max discomfort of intermittent cramps and getting up multiple times in the night to change.

But the hormones must be doing something. And they are, on the other days leading up to the actual Irma arrival. The white (”Eggy” aka ovulating days) and yellow (PMS) have more number of short bars compared to other days.

Phase Comparison

Horizontal bar chart comparing sleep metrics across the different phases of the cycle, revealing which phases bring the most restorative rest.

Only 1 of all 35 poor (under 40) score is an Irma day, whereas 25 are on a PMS or Eggy day.
7 of the 10 best (>90 score) days are on a Free day.

Eggy seems to have the most count of poor days followed by PMS, although the boundary between eggy and PMS is difficult to draw. Collectively, they claim most of the poor sleep days.

And with that we can now say for certain that Irma is released of all charges.
Sorry Irma!

Summarising what I learnt,
• There are far more good days than bad
• Consistent bed-time and waking-time improve sleep
• Weekdays are better than weekends; Summers are better than winters
• Increased outdoor physical activities improve sleep
• PMS has latent negative effect compared to actual period days

Of course, all of this analysis is speculative and there are so many variables in this equation that it cannot give a black and white to-do list. What it has given me is some debunked theories about my sleep that I was totally convinced with, and an endless curiosity to find out more variables and debunk (or hopefully bunk) more of my theories.
